Soft Subdivision Search

    Advised by: Prof. Chee Yap
    Date: Sep. 2015 - July 2017

    Motion planning is a fundamental problem in robotics. There are three main approaches:
  1. The Exact Approach which can, in principle, solve any algebraic planning problem. But practitioners tend to implement exact algorithms using machine approximations; then it is no longer clear what the guarantees of exact algorithms mean. In short, "Exact" algorithms are impractical except for the simplest cases.
  2. The Sampling Approach has been dominant in motion planning research domain for the last two decades. We refer to Probabilistic Road Map (PRM) the main representative. Its central problem is inability to halting (couched as “narrow passage problem”).
  3. The Subdivision Approach (cell decomposition) is the oldest among the three approaches, and remains popular with practitioners: our work falls under this category. We introduced the concept of resolution-exactness as the theoretical foundation for path planning that side-steps exact computation. This opens the way for the development of soft predicates, replacing the usual predicates that control the logic of all geometric algorithms.

Path Planning for Simple Robots using Soft Subdivision Search

We introduce the Soft Subdivision Search (SSS) framework for designing a new class of path planning algorithms, founded on the concept of resolution-exactness. This video illustrates the SSS framework for a trio of simple planar robots: disc, triangle and 2-links. The algorithm achieves state-of-art real-time performance, and is relatively easy to implement.

Ching-Hsiang Hsu, John Paul Ryan, and Chee Yap, "Path Planning for Simple Robots using Soft Subdivision Search," Multimedia Exposition. Proc. 32nd International Symposium on Computational Geometry, Boston, MA. June 14-18, 2016.

Resolution-Exact Planner for Thick Non-Crossing 2-Link Robots

We propose a novel and efficient planner for non-crossing 2-link thick robots. Our work contributes to the development of practical and theoretically sound subdivision planners. It is reasonable to expect a tradeoff between the stronger guarantees of our resolution-exact approach versus a faster running time for sampling approaches. But, our experiments suggest no such tradeoffs at all: SSS is consistently superior to sampling.
The sampling approaches are from Open Motion Planning Library (OMPL). In addition, I have implemented one of the latest PRM variants, Lazy Toggle PRM in the performance comparison. (For making an unbiased comparison, I would like to adopt open source library first. But, I have implemented the both PRM and RRT algorithm on my own too. Please see the RRT demonstration video with a 2-links robot in different step size.)

Chee Yap, Zhongdi Luo, and Ching-Hsiang Hsu, "Resolution-Exact Planner for Thick Non-Crossing 2-Link Robots," 12th Int'l Workshop on the Algorithmic Foundations of Robotics (WAFR 2016), Dec. 18-20, 2016

Rods and Rings: Soft Subdivision Planner for R³ x S²

We continue making progress of SSS for rods and rings in 3D, with implementations. One major technical difference from our previous work on planar robots is that we gave up the notion of "forbidden orientations" (this is quite complicated for spatial robots) but, opted for an alternative approach to bound the footprint of boxes using what we call Σ2-sets. Such sets generalize the concept of Π1-sets in our previous work. One benefit of Σ2-sets is that they are very flexible, allowing us to easily extend the planners to “thick” versions of a rod or ring. The trade-off in using Σ2-sets is a modest increase in the accuracy constant K.

Ching-Hsiang Hsu, Yi-Jen Chiang, and Chee Yap, "Soft Subdivision Planner for a Rod," Fall Workshops on Computational Geometry, Oct 27-28, 2016.
